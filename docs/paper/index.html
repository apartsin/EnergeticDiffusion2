DGLD: Domain-Gated Latent Diffusion for the Discovery of Novel Energetic Materials

Abstract

We introduce Domain-Gated Latent Diffusion (DGLD), a four-stage framework for property-targeted molecular generation. The central methodological contribution is multi-objective classifier guidance via a multi-head latent score model: a small noise-conditional MLP with six heads (viability, SA, SC, sensitivity, performance, and a literature-grounded hazard head trained on 196 (latent, h50/primary-explosive) pairs) is trained once over cached encoder posteriors and used to add per-head autograd gradients to every step of an otherwise frozen latent denoiser, exposing per-head scales \(s_k\) as a sample-time interface that acts as on/off switches over chemistry classes (per-head dose-response is saturated by the per-row gradient clamp; finer-grained scaling requires the unit-norm rebalancing variant of §4.4). We report a methodologically important configuration finding: the standard image-domain classifier-guidance recipe (alpha-anneal + tight gradient-norm clamp) silently muted the guidance signal in our small-step (\(n=40\)) frozen-decoder regime, producing bit-identical output across guidance configurations. Disabling the anneal and loosening the per-row clamp restores per-head steering: at the headline pool=40 000 four guidance conditions produce visibly distinct top-100 distributions, and the per-head scales steer chemistry class as intended (Fig. 7b, §5.5b). Three auxiliary contributions support the lift: (i) a four-tier label-trust hierarchy that lets unreliable labels participate in the unconditional prior via cfg-dropout without contaminating the conditional signal; (ii) hard-negative latent augmentation (918 cheats encoded as viab=0); and (iii) a three-layer credibility filter for top leads: a chemist-audited SMARTS catalog, an xTB HOMO–LUMO gap gate, and B3LYP/6-31G* geometry-optimisation confirmation, with the chemist-SMARTS and DFT layers shown to be complementary — DFT confirms electronic-structure-credible candidates that are real local minima while the chemist SMARTS catches motif-level shock-sensitivity risks DFT cannot predict. We instantiate DGLD on energetic materials: a LIMO VAE [1] is fine-tuned on 326 k chemistry-curated SMILES and used as a frozen encoder/decoder; a conditional latent diffusion model with classifier-free guidance [14] is conditioned on four detonation properties drawn from the tier hierarchy. For validation, the harder credibility numbers we lead with are 77/100 chemist-SMARTS-pass (post-audit catalog, §5.5c) and 5/5 DFT-validated leads + anchors with anchor-calibrated B3LYP/6-31G(d) opt + \(\omega\)B97X-D3BJ/def2-TZVP single-point energies and atomization-energy HOFs (§5.13). Complementary distributional metrics: median NN-Tanimoto 0.32 against the 694 k augmented training corpus (§5.5; 0/100 within Tani 0.70); 96/97 PubChem-novel (§5.5; necessary but partly tautological for energetic chemistry which PubChem under-indexes); 85/100 GFN2-xTB electronically credible at gap \(\ge\) 1.5 eV (a soft gating signal, not a stability verdict). Top lead is a trinitro-1,2-isoxazole on a parent scaffold previously documented in the dinitro-isoxazole literature (Klapötke [40]); 3D-CNN-predicted \(\rho=2.00\) g/cm3, \(D=9.56\) km/s, \(P=40.5\) GPa, with anchor-calibrated DFT giving \(\rho \approx 2.10\) g/cm3, HOF \(\approx +60\) kJ/mol and the calibrated K-J recompute placing absolute \(D\) 1.5–2 km/s below the 3D-CNN value (a known formula limitation in the high-N regime; population-level evidence in §5.13 D9). The relative ranking of L1 above HMX/CL-20 is preserved across both prediction routes; the absolute \(D\) on a publication-grade CHNO scale would require a composite-method or experimental confirmation.

1. Introduction

The discovery of new high-energy-density molecules (HEDMs) sits at an awkward intersection. The space of synthesisable C/H/N/O small molecules with the right balance of density, oxygen balance, heat of formation, and detonation kinetics is vast, but the available labelled data is small, expensive, and frequently inconsistent across sources. Empirical models of detonation performance (Kamlet–Jacobs [12] and its descendants) are convenient but lossy. DFT-derived heats of formation are slow and biased; experimental measurements are scarce. Recent machine-learning surrogates predict detonation properties from 3D structure with respectable accuracy [15][16][14a], but on their own they only score; they do not propose. The interesting question is whether a generative model trained on a heterogeneous, partially-mislabelled corpus of 105–106 SMILES can hit a multi-property target on the high tail of the distribution, while staying inside the manifold of synthesisable chemistry.

Two separate research threads have ripened recently. First, molecular variational autoencoders [2][3][1][4] have produced bidirectional latent spaces that are dense enough for gradient-based optimisation, and large enough that pretraining on ZINC [22] transfers to downstream tasks. Second, denoising diffusion models [18][17] with classifier-free guidance [14] have shown that conditioning at the latent level [19a] is enough to steer generation tightly toward target properties without destroying sample quality. We combine the two: a frozen, fine-tuned LIMO encoder/decoder maps SMILES to a 1024-d Gaussian latent, and a conditional DDPM with FiLM-modulated property conditioning generates new latents which the decoder lifts back to molecules.

This setup is attractive precisely because the limiting resource — reliable labels — can be partitioned. Rather than throw out the half-million unlabelled rows in our augmented corpus, we use them to train the unconditional prior and only let trustworthy labels (Tier A: experimental; Tier B: DFT) drive the conditioning signal. Lower-quality labels (Tier C: Kamlet–Jacobs derived; Tier D: 3D-CNN smoke-model surrogate) participate via masking and dropout. The result is a model that pulls hard toward an energetic distribution because most of the training data is energetic, but only takes property guidance from the slice where labels can be trusted.

Contributions.   (i) Multi-objective classifier guidance with a multi-head latent score model: the central methodological contribution. A small noise-conditional score model with separate heads for viability, synthetic accessibility, sensitivity, and target-property compliance is trained once over cached encoder posteriors and used to add per-head autograd gradients to every diffusion step. The denoiser stays frozen, so the guidance signal can be tuned and re-tuned in minutes without retraining the diffusion backbone. We show that the per-head guidance scales \(s_k\) act as interpretable knobs on the trade-off between target-property maximisation and chemical credibility, and that gentle scales (\(s_{\text{viab}}\!=\!1.0\), \(s_{\text{sens}}\!=\!0.3\)) lift the top-1 composite score by 0.05 and the top-1 detonation velocity by 0.6 km/s in matched-compute head-to-head against an unguided baseline (Fig. 7, §5.5).  (ii) A four-tier label-reliability hierarchy and a conditioning-mask recipe that lets unreliable labels participate in the unconditional prior without contaminating the conditional signal.  (iii) A domain-knowledge gating layer that combines chemistry-aware SMARTS alerts, a learned viability classifier, a sensitivity proxy, and Pareto multi-objective ranking to demote latent-space “model cheats” that the property predictor confidently scores high.  (iv) A complete pipeline that turns a heterogeneous 326 k+ SMILES corpus into a multi-property-targeted candidate list of \(O(10^3)\) novel HEDMs in roughly two hours of single-GPU compute, with a top lead at predicted density 2.00 g/cm3, detonation velocity 9.56 km/s, and pressure 40.5 GPa on a stabilised aromatic scaffold.

2. Related work

2.1 Molecular generative models: latent-space VAEs

The earliest deep generative models of molecules formulated the problem as VAE training over SMILES. Gómez-Bombarelli et al. [2] showed that a continuous latent over SMILES enables Bayesian-optimisation-style property optimisation, although the LSTM decoder produced many invalid strings. Junction-tree VAEs (JT-VAE) [3] guaranteed validity by decoding into chemical fragments. CDDD [31] learned data-driven continuous descriptors via cross-language SMILES translation. Mol-CycleGAN [32] performed property-targeted optimisation in JT-VAE latent space without an explicit property model. SELFIES [18a] sidestepped the string-validity problem entirely: every SELFIES string corresponds to a valid molecule by construction, and we adopt this representation. LIMO [1] trained an MLP-based VAE over SELFIES with a parallel non-autoregressive decoder — the architecture we fine-tune. MolMIM [4] uses a Perceiver encoder [24] and a mutual-information training objective to produce dense 512-d latents over billion-SMILES pretraining corpora.

2.2 Molecular generative models: language and graph approaches

SMILES is a string, so language modelling is a natural fit. REINVENT [28] trained a recurrent generator on SMILES and biased it toward design objectives via reinforcement learning. ChemTS [29] combined an RNN prior with Monte-Carlo tree search. MolGPT [30] applied a GPT-style decoder to SMILES with property tokens prepended for control. MoLFormer-XL [5] and Chemformer [9] showed that BERT/T5-style pretraining on hundreds of millions of SMILES yields representations that transfer to property-prediction tasks. We treat these as orthogonal alternatives but they share with us the use of large-scale pretraining as a prior. On the graph side, DiGress [8] performs discrete denoising diffusion directly on molecular graphs, avoiding tokenisation altogether; its strength is exact validity, its weakness for our setting is that property conditioning must be re-introduced via a separate guidance term.

2.3 Diffusion models and classifier-free guidance

Diffusion models [18][16a][17] have replaced GANs in many continuous-data domains and beat them outright on standard image benchmarks [15a]. Latent-space diffusion [19a] separates perceptual compression (a VAE) from prior modelling (a diffusion model in latent space) and is now the dominant recipe for text-to-image. The same factorisation works here: a pretrained molecular VAE supplies a smooth, dense latent, and a small denoiser models its prior. Classifier-free guidance [14] avoids training a separate property predictor; instead the same denoiser is trained jointly with and without conditioning, and the conditioning gradient is reconstructed at sample time as $$\hat{\epsilon}_{\theta}(z_t, c) \;=\; \epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, \emptyset) \;+\; w \cdot \big(\epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, c) - \epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, \emptyset)\big),$$ with guidance scale \(w\). The classifier-guidance ancestor [15a] requires a separately-trained noise-conditional classifier, which would be expensive in our setting because each property has its own scale and dropout pattern.

2.4 Equivariant 3D and target-conditioned molecular diffusion

For 3D molecular generation, equivariant diffusion (EDM) [7] diffuses atom positions and types under SE(3)-equivariant networks. Subsequent target-aware variants — DiffSBDD [33], TargetDiff [34], Pocket2Mol [36], and the docking-oriented DiffDock [35] — demonstrate that conditioning a diffusion model on a 3D pocket or environment is feasible and competitive with reinforcement-learning baselines on protein-ligand tasks. MolDiff [10] tackles atom-bond inconsistency in 3D diffusion. Our setting differs in that the conditioning signal is a continuous physical property of the molecule itself, not the geometry of an external receptor; latent-space diffusion is therefore the more natural fit and avoids 3D-conformer preprocessing.

2.5 Energetic-materials ML: detonation-property prediction

The Kamlet–Jacobs equations [12] remain the workhorse for fast detonation-property estimates and provide the Tier C labels in our hierarchy. Early neural models targeted impact sensitivity from molecular descriptors [39]. Modern ML surrogates have closed the gap to DFT for several relevant outputs: Casey et al. [15] train 3D-CNNs that predict detonation properties from electronic-structure-derived volumetric inputs; we use an ensemble built on top of Uni-Mol [16] as the fast post-rerank scorer. Elton et al. [14a] showed that simple ML pipelines match Kamlet–Jacobs accuracy across a broad set of CHNO compounds. Klapötke [40] reviews the synthesis chemistry of nitrogen-rich heterocycles, providing strong qualitative priors for which fragments matter; our motif-augmented training set is built on the same fragment families.

2.6 Energetic-materials ML: generative side

Most ML work on energetic materials has been discriminative: predict density, heat of formation, or detonation parameters from a labelled SMILES set. Generative work targeting the high-energy tail is comparatively new, and constrained by the small size of the labelled corpus (typically \(\le\) 104 rows). Our four-tier label hierarchy and conditioning-mask training recipe are designed precisely for this regime. The closest spiritual analogues are constrained-Bayesian-optimisation pipelines that use a VAE prior to navigate chemical space toward a property target [41]; classifier-free latent diffusion subsumes that recipe in a single end-to-end model. The performance / stability trade-off our gating layer enforces also has a discriminative-ML counterpart: Huang et al. [18b] apply ML to balance performance and stability of high-energy-density materials at the property-prediction step rather than the generation step, a complementary direction.

2.7 General-purpose property predictors

For property prediction outside the energetic-materials niche, message-passing graph networks like Chemprop / D-MPNN [42] remain a strong default. Quantum-aware models such as SchNet [43] and OrbNet [44] achieve DFT-level accuracy on energy and force predictions on small molecules at much lower cost. We do not use these directly: the smoke-model 3D-CNN ensemble was trained specifically against detonation outputs and is in-distribution for our energetic-materials task; the broader graph models would have to be re-fit to detonation labels.

2.8 Benchmarks, metrics, and synthesisability priors

The MOSES [46] and GuacaMol [45] benchmarks have standardised the evaluation of molecular generators on validity, novelty, and similarity-to-training metrics. The Fréchet ChemNet Distance [47] reports distributional similarity between generated and reference sets in a learned chemical-feature space. The chemical-space project [48] provides enumerated reference sets (GDB-13, GDB-17) that bound how much of small-molecule space remains untouched. Synthesisability is a separate concern: the SA score [19] penalises uncommon ring fusions and rare fragments and is a fast bound on accessibility; the SCScore [20] learns a complexity proxy from a reaction corpus and complements SA by rewarding fragments that appear as products. We apply both as hard caps. Tanimoto similarity [21] on Morgan fingerprints provides a simple, well-understood novelty bound against the training set; we use a window (0.20, 0.55) to keep candidates close enough to known chemistry to be plausible but far enough to be new.

3. Dataset construction

Three resources feed the training set: a labelled energetic-materials master file (\(\sim 66\,\)k canonical SMILES with at least one of {density, HOF, detonation velocity, detonation pressure}); a much larger unlabelled SMILES dump from the same domain (\(\sim 380\,\)k); and a motif-augmented expansion that systematically varies nitro/nitramine/azide/tetrazole substituents on the labelled scaffolds (\(\sim 1.08\,\)M after per-source canonicalisation and token-length filtering). All three are then merged and re-deduplicated by canonical SMILES, dropping rows that do not tokenise within the 72-token SELFIES limit of LIMO, or contain atoms outside the 108-token vocabulary. After the cross-source dedup, the final pretraining pool contains \(\sim 694\,\)k unique canonical SMILES (the corpus referenced as the “augmented training corpus” in §5.5 and §A0); the difference between the per-source \(\sim 1.08\,\)M figure and the deduplicated 694 k reflects the substantial overlap among the three sources, particularly between the labelled master and the motif-augmented expansion which by construction use overlapping scaffolds.

3.1 Four-tier label hierarchy

Each label per row is annotated with a tier:

TierSource~ rowsTrusted for conditioning?
AExperimental measurement (literature)~3 000yes
BDFT-derived (B3LYP/6-31G* heat of formation; experimentally-anchored density)~9 000yes
CKamlet–Jacobs derived from quoted density & HOF (regime-limited: reliable for carbon-deficient CHNO with \(2a + b/2 \ge d \ge b/2\); under-predicts \(D\) for high-N low-H compounds where the assumed gas-product distribution is wrong)~25 000no (mask out)
D3D-CNN smoke-model surrogate~30 000no (mask out)

For our final denoiser variants (v4-B and v3) the conditioning signal fires only on Tier-A and Tier-B rows; the rest contribute exclusively to the unconditional prior, via the cfg-dropout mechanism described in §4.2. This is the cleanest way we found to use the full corpus without letting noisy labels pollute the conditional gradient.

3.2 Encoding the corpus

We canonicalise all SMILES with RDKit [22a], convert to SELFIES, tokenise to LIMO's 108-symbol alphabet, and pass through the LIMO encoder once to obtain a deterministic latent mean \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^{1024}\) per row (we keep \(\mu\) only; the encoder posterior variance \(\sigma\) is discarded for cached latents, since the diffusion model treats each cached row as a deterministic anchor in latent space rather than as a sample from the posterior). The result is cached as latents.pt: a single tensor of \(\mu\) values plus a property matrix, a tier matrix, a per-row trust mask, and per-property normalisation statistics. The denoiser sees only this tensor; it never re-encodes anything.

3.3 High-tail oversampling

The empirical distribution of detonation velocity in the labelled subset is sharply peaked around 7–8 km/s. To bias generation toward the tail, we oversample rows whose label exceeds the 90th percentile by a factor of 5×–10×, restricted to Tier A/B. We refer to this as asymmetric high-tail oversampling; an ablation reverting it produces leads with a 0.4 km/s lower mean predicted velocity (Appendix C).

4. Methodology

4.1 LIMO fine-tuning

We start from the pretrained LIMO checkpoint distributed with [1]. The encoder maps a (B, 72) SELFIES-token tensor through a 64-dim embedding and a multilayer MLP to a (B, 1024) Gaussian latent; the decoder is a similarly-sized MLP that produces a (B, 72, 108) log-probability tensor in parallel (no autoregression). We fine-tune all parameters for ~8.5k steps on 326 k energetic-biased SMILES with the standard ELBO, $$\mathcal{L}(x) \;=\; -\mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)] \;+\; \beta \cdot \mathrm{KL}(q(z|x) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(0,I)),$$ with \(\beta=0.01\) and free-bits clipping disabled (free-bits did not improve self-consistency in our setting). After fine-tuning, validation token-accuracy is 64.5 %. The latent posterior is concentrated: \(\|\mu\|\approx 8\) on average, well below the \(\sqrt{1024}\approx 32\) expected of \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\), and we discuss in §6 how this mismatch affects diffusion-time decoding.

4.2 Conditional latent diffusion

The denoiser \(\epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, t, c, m)\) is a 44.6 M-parameter FiLM-modulated ResNet over \(z_t \in \mathbb{R}^{1024}\) with sinusoidal time embedding (\(d_t=256\)) and a property-conditioner that combines a sinusoidal value embedding (\(d_c=64\)) with the trust-mask \(m\in\{0,1\}^4\). The forward process is the standard variance-preserving DDPM [18]: $$z_t \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,z_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I), \quad t\in\{1,\ldots,T=1000\}.$$ The training loss is the masked MSE between predicted and true noise, dropping conditioning with probability 0.10 to fit the unconditional branch. Training is 20 epochs, batch 128, AdamW with peak LR \(10^{-4}\), cosine decay, EMA decay 0.999.

At sample time we use the standard guided-sampling step, $$\hat\epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, c) \;=\; \epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, t, \emptyset) \;+\; w\,\big(\epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, t, c) - \epsilon_{\theta}(z_t, t, \emptyset)\big),$$ with guidance scale \(w\in\{5,7,9\}\). Pushing \(w\) higher concentrates the empirical sample distribution near the targets but reduces the diversity of returned candidates after filtering, as Figure 3 shows.

4.3 Denoiser ensemble (production)

The production sampler ensembles two complementary denoisers trained with different oversampling schedules. v4-B applies Tier-A/B-only conditioning with +5× high-tail oversampling (top-decile values), weighted_mask=true, and property-dropout 0.30; it excels at meeting the density, detonation-velocity, and detonation-pressure targets simultaneously. v3 uses asymmetric high-tail oversampling restricted to Tier-A/B, factor 5×, focused on the heat-of-formation tail. Sampling each independently and merging candidate pools (followed by canonical-SMILES deduplication) recovers candidates that neither model alone would produce: v3 contributes the majority of high-HOF leads, while v4-B contributes the bulk of high-density / high-pressure leads.

4.4 Multi-objective classifier guidance at sample time

Classifier-free guidance steers generation toward target property values, but it does not directly enforce the chemistry priors that distinguish a viable energetic from a model-cheat polynitro fragment. We add a second guidance signal at sample time, in the spirit of classifier guidance [15a], but multi-objective: a small noise-conditional score model with several heads is trained once on cached encoder posteriors, and at every diffusion step its per-head gradient with respect to \(z_t\) is added to the denoiser's noise estimate. Crucially, the denoiser itself is left frozen.

Architecture. A 4-block FiLM-conditioned MLP (input \(z\in\mathbb{R}^{1024}\) and the diffusion noise level \(\sigma\) embedded into 128-d), with five heads that share a 1024-d trunk: viability (sigmoid logit), SA (regression to z-scored SA score), SC (regression to z-scored SCScore), sensitivity (regression to a heuristic [0,1] proxy), and performance (4-d regression to standardised \(\rho\)/HOF/\(D\)/\(P\)). \(\sigma\) is injected via FiLM scale-and-shift on every block, so the model's response is genuinely noise-aware rather than treating \(\sigma\) as just another input dimension.

Training labels. For every cached training latent we set the viability target to $$y_{\text{viab}}(z) \;=\; \mathbf{1}[\,\text{redflags.pass}(\text{decode}(z))\,] \cdot P_{\text{RF}}(\text{viable}\mid \text{decode}(z)),$$ where \(P_{\text{RF}}\) is a RandomForest classifier (Morgan FP + RDKit descriptors, validation AUC 0.999) trained against ZINC-15 negatives plus 137 hard negatives mined from earlier generation rounds. Multiplying by the SMARTS-screen pass / fail floor is essential: without it, the score model learns to score gem-dinitro 4-rings and polyazene chains identically to RDX, because the encoder maps both to nearby latent regions. SA and SC labels come from RDKit and the SCScorer; sensitivity labels come from the heuristic in §4.5; performance labels come from the labelled training rows with the same Tier-A/B mask used for the denoiser.

Hard-negative augmentation (self-distillation). Hard negatives are mined from the model's own prior generation pools and are therefore a self-distillation signal: each generation cycle, the gated-but-failing tail of decoded candidates (small-skeleton polynitro, polyazene chains, gem-tetranitro on C2–C3, trinitromethane variants) is harvested, canonicalised, and re-encoded through the LIMO encoder as additional viab=0 training latents. The score-model variants in this paper correspond to three rounds of self-distillation: v2 (137 hard negatives, mined from the cycle-3 pool), v3d (918 hard negatives, accumulated through cycle-7), v3e (same 918 + retrained SC head). Each round we monitor a held-out anchor/cheat probe (7 anchors: RDX, HMX, TNT, FOX-7, PETN, TATB, NTO; 5 cheats: gem-tetranitro on C2, trinitromethane, gem-dinitrocyclopropane, polyazene-NO2 chain, dinitromethane) to confirm the round did not regress on anchors; we stop iterating when anchor scores stay at 0.86–0.99 and cheats are demoted to 0.12–0.84 (achieved at v3d). Without this step the score model is non-discriminative on held-out cheats; with it, the gradient through the viability head reliably steers away from polynitro-on-small-skeleton chemistry.

Sensitivity head: heuristic vs literature-grounded variants. The reported v3e score model trains the sensitivity head against the hand-coded heuristic of §4.5 (nitro density, open-chain N-NO2 count, small-skeleton penalties), then z-scored. Because guiding generation with gradients of a head trained on a heuristic is partially tautological as a measurement of sensitivity reduction, we additionally fine-tune a literature-grounded variant (score_model_v3e_h50): the trunk and the four other heads are frozen at v3e weights, the sensitivity head is re-initialised, and the head is fit on 306 (canonical SMILES, observed h50) pairs from the Huang & Massa compilation [58a] mapped to a sigmoid sensitivity proxy in [0, 1] (h50=40 cm pivot, slope 1.5 in log-h50 units). Training mixes a smooth-L1 loss on the 276-row h50 train split with a teacher regulariser pulling the new head toward the v3e-old-head prediction on 383 k auxiliary latents, weighted 1.0:0.25. The retrained head reaches Pearson r=+0.71 on a 30-row held-out h50 split and produces qualitatively correct relative orderings on a 5-anchor smoke test (TATB, h50~490 cm, scored 0.37; trinitro-isoxazole lead scored 0.55). The literature-grounded variant is a drop-in replacement for v3e at sample time and is exposed via the --score_model argument of the joint reranker.

Sample-time gradient. At each DDIM step \(t\) with \(\bar\alpha_t\), define \(\sigma_t=\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\). The guided noise estimate is $$\hat\epsilon = \epsilon_\theta^{\text{cfg}}(z_t, t, c) \;-\; \sigma_t \sum_{k\in\{\text{viab},\text{sens},\text{SA},\text{hazard}\}} s_k\,\nabla_{z_t}\,\mathcal{L}_k\big(z_t,\sigma_t\big),$$ where \(\epsilon_\theta^{\text{cfg}}\) is the standard classifier-free-guidance noise estimate over the frozen denoisers, and the per-head losses are \(-\log P_\text{viab}\) (ascend), \(\mathrm{sens}_z\) (descend), \(\mathrm{SA}_z\) (descend), and \(-\log(1-P_\text{hazard})\) (descend hazard). The default scales are \(s_{\text{viab}}=1.0\), \(s_{\text{sens}}=0.3\), \(s_{\text{hazard}}=1.0\), \(s_{\text{SA}}=0\).

Configuration finding (important). An initial round of head-to-head experiments (4 conditions \(\times\) 3 seeds at pool=20 000 each) produced bit-identical SMILES distributions across all guidance configurations. Diagnostic per-step logging revealed that the standard image-domain classifier-guidance recipe — an alpha-anneal of the form \(\text{anneal}(t)=\max(0, 1-\sigma_t/\sigma_{\max})\) combined with a tight per-row gradient-norm clamp at 5.0 — zeros the gradient at high noise levels (anneal \(\to\) 0 when \(\sigma_t \to \sigma_{\max}\), i.e.\ at the start of the DDIM trajectory) and clamps legitimate signal at higher head-scales. Combined, these two safeguards muted the guidance signal so completely that the diffusion sampler followed the unguided trajectory regardless of the per-head scales \(s_k\). After disabling the anneal (\(\sigma_{\max}\!=\!0\) flag) and raising the clamp to 50, the four guidance conditions produce visibly different chemistry distributions (different MD5, different file sizes, different top-1 SMILES). We report this as a methodological caveat: the alpha-anneal recipe imported from image-domain classifier guidance is unsuited to the small-step (\(n=40\)), frozen-decoder (LIMO) regime; the workable configuration is to apply guidance uniformly across all DDIM steps with a generous per-row gradient clamp.

Per-head gradient-norm rebalancing (optional). Different heads naturally produce different gradient magnitudes (sigmoid logits \(\sim\)0.5; smooth-L1 z-scores \(\sim\)2.0); the raw per-head scales \(s_k\) therefore conflate "head importance" with "head gradient magnitude." We add an optional unit-norm rebalancing: each head's gradient is normalised per-row before scaling, so \(s_k\) cleanly controls steering weight. This makes the head-scale ablation cleanly interpretable; it is a no-op when only one head is active.

4.5 Re-ranking pipeline

Each pool is canonicalised, deduplicated, and stripped of charged species and over-large molecules. Surviving rows are scored by the 3D-CNN smoke ensemble (8 outputs: density, det-velocity, det-pressure, det-temperature, det-energy, det-volume, HOF, BDE) and a chemistry filter that removes radicals, sulphur and halogens, mixed valence states, and other red flags. SA [19] and SCScore [20] are computed and capped at 5.0 and 3.5 respectively. Caveat (drug-like provenance): both SA and SCScore are trained on drug-like reaction corpora (Reaxys, pharma chemistry); they penalise uncommon ring fusions and rare fragments, including some that are well-precedented in energetic-materials synthesis (CL-20-class polyaza-cages, hexanitrobenzene). For the present paper we accept this conservative bias because our targets are sub-CL-20 in scaffold complexity, but a future iteration should replace these with energetic-domain accessibility metrics (e.g., a literature-based "appears in published energetic syntheses" classifier). Tanimoto similarity to the nearest training-set neighbour is required to lie within \([0.20, 0.55]\): close enough to be plausible, far enough to be novel. The chemistry filter is implemented twice in the repository: a hand-coded SMARTS module (chem_redflags.py, used to produce all numbers reported in this paper) and a YAML-driven equivalent (rules/alerts.yaml + scoring_framework.py) that replicates the same accept/reject behaviour but exposes every rule, demerit, and threshold as a versioned configuration file. The production reranker (rerank_v2.py) accepts a --use_yaml_screen flag to switch backends; we keep the legacy backend as default to preserve reproducibility of the merged top-100 result, and recommend the YAML backend for future cycles. The composite score is $$s(x) \;=\; \sum_{k\in\{\rho,\,\mathrm{HOF},\,D,\,P\}} \alpha_k \cdot \frac{|\hat y_k(x) - y_k^*|}{\sigma_k},$$ where \(y_k^*\) is the design target and \(\sigma_k\) is the per-property training standard deviation. Lower is better. The candidates returned to the user are the lowest-\(s\) retained.

Targets used throughout: \(\rho\!=\!1.95\) g/cm3, HOF\(=\!+220\) kJ/mol, \(D\!=\!9.5\) km/s, \(P\!=\!40\) GPa. SA \(\le 5.0\), SC \(\le 3.5\), Tanimoto window \([0.20, 0.55]\), neutral molecules only. 5.1 Pool-size scaling

The simplest knob in this pipeline is the size of the candidate pool drawn from the diffusion model. Figure 4 shows that increasing the pool from 1.5 k to 40 k drops the best composite score by ~5× and increases the number of candidates surviving every filter by more than 20×. Returns are still positive at 40 k; we expect 80–160 k pools to extract additional novel scaffolds.

5.2 Multi-objective gated reranking

Single-objective composite scoring is unreliable for energetic-materials generation: the predictor can be exploited by candidates whose predicted properties exceed the training-distribution support. We therefore apply a gated, multi-objective reranker on the candidate pool before reporting leads. Hard filters reject (i) gem-tetranitro and other model-cheat SMARTS patterns, (ii) MW < 130 Da, (iii) nitro/heavy-atom ratio > 0.42, (iv) oxygen balance > +25 %, (v) net non-zero charge, and (vi) ≥ 3 nitro groups on a one- or two-carbon skeleton. Surviving candidates are scored by

$$S(x) \;=\; 0.45\, S_{\text{perf}}^{\text{band}}(x) \;+\; 0.20\, S_{\text{viab}}(x) \;+\; 0.15\, S_{\text{novel}}(x) \;+\; 0.20\,\big(1-S_{\text{sens}}(x)\big) \;-\; 0.10\, S_{\text{alerts}}(x),$$

where \(S_{\text{perf}}^{\text{band}}\) is a saturating banded ramp on each property (capped at 1.0 above the design band, so the model cannot win by extrapolation), \(S_{\text{viab}}\) is the output of a Random-Forest classifier trained to discriminate the 66 k labelled energetic compounds from 80 k random ZINC samples (validation AUC 0.9986), \(S_{\text{novel}}\) is the inverse-Tanimoto novelty in the (0.20, 0.55) window, and \(S_{\text{sens}}\) penalises open-chain N-NO2 motifs and small-skeleton polynitro patterns. Final ranking is the Pareto front over (perf, viab) maximisation × (sens, alerts) minimisation, broken by composite score.

Of the top-400 candidates from the pool=40 000 single-objective rerank, 45 are rejected by the hard filters: 11 with three nitro groups on a two-carbon chain, four with three nitros on a one-carbon centre, 28 with MW below 130, and two with oxygen balance above +25 %. The 355 survivors define a Pareto front of 34 candidates.

5.5b Multi-head guidance head-to-head (patched configuration)

To isolate the contribution of multi-head classifier guidance from the rest of the pipeline, we ran a four-condition head-to-head at matched compute (pool=40 000 each, identical CFG=7, identical DDIM steps=40, identical Phase-A reranker, single seed) on a rented RTX 4090. The four conditions are:

  • C0 unguided: classifier-free guidance only.
  • C1 viab+sens: \(s_{\text{viab}}=1.0\), \(s_{\text{sens}}=0.3\) (paper default).
  • C2 viab+sens+hazard: \(s_{\text{viab}}=1.0\), \(s_{\text{sens}}=0.3\), \(s_{\text{hazard}}=1.0\) (default with v3f hazard head).
  • C3 hazard-only: \(s_{\text{hazard}}=2.0\), other heads off (probe of the hazard signal in isolation).

An initial round of this ablation (with the default classifier-guidance recipe inherited from image-domain literature) produced bit-identical SMILES across all four conditions: identical MD5, identical file sizes, identical top-100. A per-step gradient-norm trace (D1 of our diagnostic; see Appendix) revealed the cause: the alpha-anneal of the form \(\text{anneal}(t)=\max(0, 1-\sigma_t/\sigma_{\max})\) with default \(\sigma_{\max}=2.0\) zeros the gradient at the start of the DDIM trajectory (when \(\sigma_t\!\to\!\sigma_{\max}\)) and the per-row clamp at \(\|g\|=5\) further compresses the late-step signal. After disabling the anneal and loosening the clamp to 50, the four conditions produce visibly distinct chemistry distributions (different MD5 hashes, different file sizes 1.67–1.84 MB across conditions, different top-1 SMILES). The patched-configuration head-to-head numbers (top-1 composite, top-1 \(D\), top-100 mean \(D\), Bemis–Murcko scaffold count, post-Phase-A keep rate, chemist-SMARTS pass rate per condition) are reported in Fig. 7b after running the local 3DCNN+Phase-A pipeline on the four 80 000-row SMILES files.

Note on the bug discovery. The standard image-domain alpha-anneal recipe is appropriate for image diffusion where the score-model classifier is more robust to high-noise inputs and the trajectory is hundreds of steps long; in our regime (40 DDIM steps, frozen LIMO decoder, score-model trained on cached posteriors at fixed sigma) the anneal compresses the entire useful gradient window into the last few steps when \(z\) is already nearly determined. Our diagnostic protocol (per-step gradient-norm logging, score-model output sensitivity to \(z\), final-z cosine similarity across conditions, decoder argmax-basin width) is included in the supplementary code as diag_guidance.py. We recommend other authors apply this checklist when porting classifier guidance to discrete-decoder latent diffusion.

5.5 Novelty

Headline result for the merged paper-ready set (top-100 by composite v2 across four pools; 331 unique candidates pre-filter): 96 of 97 net are absent from PubChem [23] (PUG REST on canonical SMILES; 3 queries returned transient REST errors and are excluded from the denominator; 1 known CID, 1-nitro-1H-tetrazol-1-amine, surfaced at rank 56). On the merged top-100, the xTB-GFN2 stability gate (HOMO-LUMO gap \(\ge\) 1.5 eV) accepts 85, including the trinitro-isoxazole at 2.61 eV; 9 candidates fall in the 1.0–1.5 eV electronically-marginal band and 2 below 1.0 eV (likely decomposition prone). The intersection of the PubChem-novelty result and the xTB stability gate is a 12-candidate set that is simultaneously absent from the largest public chemical database, predicted to satisfy four detonation-property targets at HMX/CL-20 levels, and electronically stable at the GFN2 level. The earlier-iteration anchors that occasionally surfaced in pre-Phase-A runs (dinitramide, 1,2-dinitrohydrazine, N,N′-dinitrocarbodiimide) are now demoted out of the gated top by the MW \(\ge\) 130 cap and the polynitro-on-small-skeleton rule. On the final merged top-100 (89 from the unguided pool=40 000 Phase-A reranker, 5 from the unguided pool=80 000 run, 3 from the multi-head guided pool=40 000 run, and 3 from the SA-gradient \(s_{\text{SA}}=0.15\) guided run): 96/97 PubChem-novel after dropping 3 rows for which the PubChem REST API returned transient errors during querying (which we exclude from the denominator rather than guess); the remaining 1/97 known-PubChem-CID is 1-nitro-1H-tetrazol-1-amine, a sanity-check rediscovery. The four findings from this sweep are: (1) the score-model heads behave as a steering interface for chemistry, not just a metric, in the sense that increasing \(s_{\text{SA}}\) shifts the explored scaffold class entirely; (2) classifier-free conditioning and classifier guidance are not interchangeable, and aggressive sensitivity guidance can destructively interfere with the CFG signal so that the sampler retreats to the unguided distribution; (3) per-head classifier-guidance gradients give a finer-grained interface than a single CFG scalar — multi-objective conditioning has been studied in the conditional-diffusion literature (e.g. [15a]); the contribution here is exposing one tunable scale per chemistry-domain head (viability, SA, SC, sensitivity) trained from a single multi-head MLP; and (4) the optimal scales identified here (\(1.0, 0.3, 0\)) are conservative and could be refined per-target by running this ablation as a small inner loop before each major generation, at \(\sim\)1 minute per condition.

5.7 Hard-negative augmentation is required for guidance to discriminate

The viability head is the gradient signal that pulls samples away from chem-cheating regions. We trained two variants of the multi-head model on identical noised-latent inputs:

  • v2: training labels = \(\mathbf{1}[\text{redflag pass}] \cdot P_{\text{RF}}\) on the labelled corpus + 137 mined hard negatives encoded as viab=0.
  • v3d: same recipe with 918 mined hard negatives (mined from all generation pools to date) and an additional aromatic-heterocycle boost \(\times 1.20\) on the viability target.

On a held-out probe of seven anchor energetics (RDX, HMX, TNT, FOX-7, PETN, TATB) and five known model-cheats (gem-tetranitro on C2; trinitromethane; gem-dinitro-cyclopropane; polyazene-NO2 chain; tiny dinitromethane), the v2 model gave anchors viability \(\in\) [0.93, 0.99] and cheats \(\in\) [0.20, 0.84], while v3d gave anchors \(\in\) [0.86, 0.99] and cheats \(\in\) [0.12, 0.84]. Both models discriminate, but v3d demoted the worst-offender model-cheat (gem-tetranitro) by an additional 0.10 absolute. The lesson is that the score-model viability head's discriminative power scales directly with the number of structurally diverse hard negatives encoded as training latents; the standard ZINC drug-like negatives we used to train the SMILES-space classifier are not enough — they are too far from the encoder's posterior to teach a useful gradient.

5.9 Stability rate scales with pool size and Phase-A discipline

Repeating the xTB triage on the gated top-15 of an unguided pool=80 000 run (using the Phase-A scaffold-aware composite for ranking), 13 of the 15 survived the 1.5 eV HOMO-LUMO gap gate; the two electronically-marginal candidates were a strained bicyclic-tetra-nitro-nitramine (gap 1.45 eV) and an open-chain hydrazone-imine chain (gap 1.43 eV). Compared to the cycle-8 (pool=40 000 + multi-head guidance + Phase-A) result of 5/8, the larger unguided pool achieved a substantially higher physical-stability rate. The interpretation is that classifier guidance can drive the sampler into a mode where individual top-of-funnel candidates score high in our learned proxies but fail at the level of frontier-orbital electronic stability, whereas a larger unguided pool with the same gating layer applied post-hoc produces a less aggressive but more physically-credible final set.

5.11 Per-pool reproducibility and the need for the Phase A scaffold composite

During development we observed that a single guidance configuration (\(s_{\text{viab}}=1.0, s_{\text{sens}}=0.3, s_{\text{SA}}=0.3\) on score-model v3d) produced a top-1 composite of 0.91 in a pool=5 000 ablation but only 0.60–0.63 when scaled to pool=20 000 / pool=40 000. The 0.91 outlier was a small isoxazole-N-oxide (\(\rho=1.89, D=9.28\) km/s) that did not re-emerge at the larger sample size. We attribute this to sampling stochasticity rather than to a real configuration win: the per-head guidance scale tunes the distribution of generated chemistry, but specific top-1 candidates are subject to the same combinatorial rarity that motivated the pool-size scaling experiments in §5.1. The empirically robust configuration combines (i) a moderate guidance signal (\(s_{\text{viab}}=1.0, s_{\text{sens}}=0.3\), no SA), (ii) a generous pool (\(\ge\) 40 000), and (iii) the Phase A perf-gated, scaffold-aware composite (§4.5) for the final ranking. SA-gradient guidance broadens the explored scaffold class, but the gain is realised most reliably as a complementary run rather than as a default scale.

Anchor calibration. The atomization-energy method at B3LYP/6-31G(d) is known to carry a systematic bias of \(\sim\)10 kJ/mol per heavy atom for CHNO molecules, accumulating to \(\sim\)200–250 kJ/mol for our compounds. The RDX/TATB anchors confirm this directly: experimental HOF for RDX is \(+70\) kJ/mol, for TATB \(-141\) kJ/mol; our \(\omega\)B97X-D3BJ values give \(+240\) and \(+83\) respectively, a systematic offset of \(+170\) (RDX) to \(+224\) (TATB) kJ/mol. The \(\rho_{\text{DFT}}\) estimator is similarly biased: experimental densities of 1.806 (RDX) and 1.937 (TATB) versus our 1.63 and 1.66, an offset of 9–14 %, attributable to the fixed packing coefficient 0.69 (real packing ranges 0.65–0.78). We apply a linear two-anchor calibration: \(\rho_{\text{cal}} = 1.136 \cdot \rho_{\text{DFT}}\) and HOF\(_{\text{cal}}\) = HOF\(_{\text{DFT}}\) \(- 197\) kJ/mol. After calibration, the top-10 chem-pass leads have \(\rho_{\text{cal}} = 1.84\)–\(2.05\) g/cm3 (RDX–CL-20 range) and HOF\(_{\text{cal}}\) = \(-364\) to \(+339\) kJ/mol — physically reasonable for the trained CHNO distribution. L1 (trinitro-isoxazole) anchor-calibrated values are \(\rho \approx 2.05\) g/cm3 and HOF \(\approx +33\) kJ/mol — within \(\sim\)2 % of the predicted \(\rho=2.00\) g/cm3; the HOF differs from the predicted +144 kJ/mol by \(\sim\)110 kJ/mol, within the residual atomization-method uncertainty after a 2-anchor calibration. The calibrated DFT broadly corroborates the predicted detonation properties of the top lead but exposes a notable HOF discrepancy that a future composite-method calibration (G4 or CBS-QB3) should resolve.

Limitation of the 2-anchor calibration. A two-point linear fit (RDX, TATB) leaves zero degrees of freedom and therefore zero residual diagnostic; the slope and intercept are forced through the two anchors with no internal estimate of fit quality. The defensible numerical band on the underlying systematic bias must therefore be drawn from the literature rather than from this calibration. For B3LYP/6-31G(d) atomization-energy HOFs on CHNO compounds, the GMTKN55 thermochemistry benchmarks of Goerigk et al. [Goerigk2017] place the systematic atomization-energy bias of B3LYP with a double-zeta polarised basis at approximately 5–15 kJ/mol per atom, which scales to \(\sim\)100–250 kJ/mol at the molecular level for our 15–25-atom leads, consistent with the +170 to +224 kJ/mol offsets we observe at the RDX/TATB anchors. Casey et al. [15] report B3LYP/6-31G* HOF residuals on a CHNO test set with molecule-level RMSE in the \(\sim\)30–50 kJ/mol band (\(\sim\)5–10 kJ/mol per heavy atom), which is the closest published analogue to our setup and which we adopt as the residual-uncertainty band on calibrated HOFs in §5.13. For density, the Bondi van-der-Waals volume estimator [Bondi1964] requires a packing coefficient that ranges 0.65–0.78 across observed CHNO crystal structures; our fixed value of 0.69 sits near the middle of this range, and the anchor-calibrated \(\rho\) would shift by approximately \(\pm 0.15\) g/cm3 across the full packing-coefficient interval, which dominates the \(\rho_{\text{cal}}\) uncertainty for any single lead.

We report the 2-anchor calibration here for transparency: it is the calibration we actually ran, and the offsets \(\rho_{\text{factor}}=1.136\) and HOF\(_{\text{offset}}=-197\) kJ/mol fall inside the literature bias bands quoted above. A 6-anchor extension (HMX, PETN, FOX-7, NTO added to RDX, TATB) is recommended for the next revision; with six points we would be able to report a residual standard deviation rather than a fixed-line correction, and to disentangle the density-functional bias from the packing-coefficient bias with separate intercept and slope diagnostics. Pending that extension, the calibrated DFT numbers in §5.13 should be read as ranking-grade rather than absolute-value-grade, with the residual atomization-method uncertainty quantified as approximately 5–15 kJ/mol per atom for B3LYP/6-31G(d) HOFs based on the Goerigk and Casey benchmarks cited above.

Caveat on absolute magnitude: this open-form K-J implementation uses \(Q \approx \Delta H_f / M_W\) without subtracting product-side enthalpies, which inflates the absolute residual relative to the closed-form K-J variant in the per-lead table above; the trend across N-fraction is the diagnostic signal, not the absolute residual values. The direction of the §5.13 attribution — that the per-lead K-J vs 3D-CNN gap is dominated by the K-J formula's regime limitations rather than by the inputs — is consistent with the population-level monotone N-fraction trend.

Thermal correction to 298 K. All HOFs above are reported at 0 K (atomization-energy method gives \(\Delta H_f^{0\,\text{K}}\)). The thermal correction \(H(298)-H(0)\) for an isolated nonlinear ideal-gas molecule is dominated by 4 RT (translational + rotational + PV term \(=\) \(\sim\)9.9 kJ/mol) plus a small low-frequency-vibration contribution (each mode \(<200\) cm−1 contributes \(\sim\)RT \(=\) 2.5 kJ/mol). For our top-10 leads with 0–6 low-frequency modes each, \(H(298)-H(0)\) ranges 10–25 kJ/mol — smaller than the residual \(\sim\)50–100 kJ/mol uncertainty from the 2-anchor calibration of the atomization energy. We report 0 K HOFs throughout for consistency; converting to 298 K standard HOF would shift values uniformly by ~10–25 kJ/mol with no impact on relative rankings or the K-J residual.

DFT functional and basis-set choice. We use B3LYP/6-31G(d) for geometry optimisation and \(\omega\)B97X-D3BJ/def2-TZVP for the single-point energy on the optimised geometry. The B3LYP/6-31G(d) geometry is a long-standing standard for CHNO molecules (Casey et al. [15] use the same level for the 3D-CNN training labels we calibrate against), and the \(\omega\)B97X-D3BJ functional is recommended for energetic-materials thermochemistry by recent benchmarks (Goerigk et al., 2017). Range-separated hybrids with empirical D3 dispersion outperform pure DFT-D2 (B3LYP-D2) and meta-GGA functionals (M06-2X) on CHNO atomization-energy benchmarks. Functionals such as \(\omega\)B97M-V or B97M-V might offer marginal further accuracy gains (\(\sim\)1–2 kJ/mol per atom) but are not yet supported in the gpu4pyscf release we used; we list this as an explicit limitation. The basis def2-TZVP is the standard balanced triple-zeta with polarisation; basis-set extrapolation to def2-QZVPP would reduce the residual basis-set incompleteness error by \(\sim\)50 % but doubles the compute time and was deferred for this revision.

The two screens (chemist SMARTS audit + DFT triage) are complementary, not redundant: DFT confirms electronic-structure-credible candidates that are real local minima; the chemist SMARTS audit catches the subset of those that have hazardous motifs known empirically to detonate or decompose under handling. We therefore report a 3-layer credibility filter for top leads: (i) chemist SMARTS pass; (ii) xTB HOMO-LUMO gap \(\ge\) 1.5 eV; (iii) DFT real-minimum confirmation. The principal limitation of this DFT validation is the same as our headline pipeline: it confirms electronic structure and frontier-orbital stability but does not capture impact sensitivity, friction, or thermal triggers, which depend on weakest-bond chemistry, crystal packing, and bond dissociation energies that are out of scope for a fast B3LYP-level screen. A composite-method (G4 or CBS-QB3) HOF for the top three leads is the next-step DFT investment we recommend, at \(\sim\)6–24 hr CPU per molecule.

5.12 Statistical reporting and limitations

We are explicit about what is and is not statistically established here, so the reader can calibrate the headline numbers correctly:

  • Single denoiser training. The reported v3 and v4-B denoisers are each one training run; we do not report variance across denoiser seeds. The denoisers are each \(\sim 6\) hr of single-GPU training, so a 3-seed sweep is feasible and is the most important next experiment.
  • Single sampling seed for most ablations. Tables in §5.4, §5.6, §5.7 report a single sampling seed per condition. The §5.11 disclosure (pool=5k top-1 composite 0.91 not reproducing at pool=40k) shows that top-1 statistics at small pool size are sampling-noise-dominated; we therefore prefer top-5 or top-100 aggregate metrics where possible. The merged top-100 result (§5.10) aggregates four pools and is far less seed-sensitive than individual top-1 numbers. For the camera-ready revision we additionally ran a matched-compute 4-condition \(\times\) 3-seed head-to-head with a no-diffusion SMILES-LSTM baseline at pool=10 000 each (on a rented RTX 4090); the results are reported in §5.5d. The patched-configuration single-seed pool=40 000 head-to-head is in §5.5b. The two are complementary: §5.5d quantifies seed variance and the diffusion-vs-LM contribution at small pool, §5.5b reports the absolute Phase-A numbers at the larger pool we use for the merged top-100.
  • No held-out test split. Our diffusion model trains on the full 694 k augmented corpus with cfg-dropout per the four-tier label-trust hierarchy (§3); we do not maintain a separate held-out test set, since the candidate-discovery objective is to extrapolate off the labelled distribution rather than recover held-out labels. The Tanimoto-stratified novelty test against the 694 k corpus (§5.5: median NN-Tanimoto 0.32; 0/100 within Tani 0.70; 0 exact matches) is our train-leakage proxy. The 1/97 PubChem-resolvable hit and 3/100 labelled-master rediscoveries (§5.5) are sanity-check rediscoveries of established CHNO motifs, not leakage of held-out test rows.
  • Validation chain is layered surrogates. Detonation properties are predicted by a 3D-CNN trained on the labelled corpus (Casey-style); xTB-GFN2 provides electronic-stability triage. Neither is a substitute for first-principles DFT validation or experimental synthesis. We position the paper as a methodology and a candidate-list paper, not a synthesis paper.
  • PubChem novelty has known holes. PubChem under-indexes energetic chemistry; the 96/97 PubChem-novel result is a necessary but not sufficient novelty test. The Tanimoto-stratified test against our 694 k augmented training corpus (§5.5, median NN-Tanimoto 0.32; 0/100 within Tanimoto 0.70) is the stronger novelty bound.
  • Sensitivity proxy. The default v3e sens head trains against a hand-coded heuristic of nitro density and chain length; the literature-grounded v3e_h50 head trains against 306 (SMILES, h50) pairs from the Huang & Massa compilation and reaches val Pearson r = +0.71 on a 30-row held-out split. Both are imperfect proxies for true impact sensitivity, which depends on weakest-bond chemistry and crystal packing in addition to electronic structure.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity not exhaustively characterised. cfg-dropout (0.10), property-dropout (0.30), oversample factor (10\(\times\)), and the Tanimoto window (0.20, 0.55) were tuned during development but no full grid is reported.
  • Headline-result attribution. 89/100 of the merged top-100 came from a single unguided pool reranked by the Phase-A scaffold composite. The original §5.6 head-sweep numbers (composite +0.05, \(D\) +0.6 km/s) were obtained with the buggy alpha-anneal configuration and over-state guidance contribution at small pool sizes. The patched-configuration head-to-head at pool=40 000 (§5.5b) provides the correct guidance-vs-unguided comparison. Multi-head guidance is a sample-time interface onto chemistry class; its post-patch contribution to the headline number is reported in §5.5b.

5.14 Hazard head as a post-hoc filter

An adjunct use of the multi-head score model is at rerank time rather than sample time: encoding each candidate SMILES through the LIMO encoder and scoring its hazard-head sigmoid output gives a soft-rejection signal that is independent of (and complementary to) the chemist-audited SMARTS catalog. We applied the v3f hazard head post-hoc to the merged top-100 and obtained the following alignment:

The hazard head recovers all 23 chemist-SMARTS rejects with perfect recall on the merged top-100 (the chemist-rejects were in the head's training-positive set, so this is partly by construction); a more honest generalisation estimate via 5-fold cross-validation across all 196 hazard-dataset rows gives mean held-out AUROC = 0.91 ± 0.05, indicating the head genuinely discriminates hazardous vs safe latent-space chemistry rather than overfitting to memorised positives. The head also flags 66 additional candidates from the merged top-100 that the SMARTS catalog let through. Manual inspection of the top-flagged candidates shows a mix of true positives (motifs the hand-written SMARTS rules don't cover but which are similar in latent space to known hazardous chemistry) and false positives (over-conservative latent-neighborhood matches: e.g., the labelled-master compound 1-nitro-1H-tetrazol-1-amine surfaces at \(P_{\text{hazard}}=0.98\) despite being a published HEDM, suggesting the head learned a broader "high N-density nitramine" feature). We therefore recommend the hazard head as a soft re-rank weight, not a hard reject. Threshold \(P_{\text{hazard}}>0.9\) reduces the flag count to a more reasonable \(\sim\)50/100; threshold \(P_{\text{hazard}}>0.5\) over-rejects.

Why this matters for the methodology. The chemist SMARTS catalog and the hazard head are complementary in a useful way: the SMARTS catalog encodes known motif-level hazards (a hard interpretable rule), while the hazard head encodes latent-space proximity to hazardous chemistry (a soft generalisation that can flag motifs the rule writer didn't anticipate). Future work should use the hazard head's "additional flags" to mine new SMARTS rules — closing the loop between the data-driven and the rule-driven safety layers.

It is not evidence that L4 and L5 are unsynthesisable; it is evidence that a generative model proposing energetic-domain chemistry needs an energetics-domain template database for retrosynthetic auditing — a gap we flag for the community.

For the present paper this means: the retrosynthetic plausibility check is a positive lower-bound for L1 (aromatic poly-nitro chemistry is well-covered by drug-domain templates) and is uninformative (rather than negative) for L4/L5. We recommend the L1 4-step route as the most actionable wet-lab target from the cycle-8 top-8.

6. Discussion

6.1 What we did not try and why

Equivariant 3D diffusion [7] would let us condition directly on the 3D charge-density input that the smoke model expects, but requires conformer generation as a preprocessing step and gives up the closed-form invertibility of the SMILES↔latent map that the LIMO decoder provides. Discrete graph diffusion [8] avoids the SELFIES tokenisation entirely but, in our preliminary experiments, did not converge on the small labelled subset within our compute budget. A GFlowNet [6] with an explicit reward over composite score would be a natural alternative to classifier-free guidance and is the most promising direction we did not pursue.

6.2 Limitations

The smoke-model surrogate is what it says on the tin: a fast 8-output predictor that is well-calibrated on the labelled distribution but extrapolates onto the high-density tail with unknown reliability. The top leads in §5.2 should be treated as candidates for DFT validation, not as final answers. In particular, lead #2's predicted \(D=9.80\) km/s is drawn from a region of (density, oxygen balance) where the smoke model has training support but where calibration was not separately verified; we report its position relative to HMX/CL-20 as a relative claim. Synthesisability bounds via SA and SCScore are heuristic; a real reaction-tree analysis is the next-step external validation.

K-J versus thermochemical-equilibrium codes. The 1.5–2 km/s K-J–vs–3D-CNN residual we report in §5.13 is consistent with the typical disagreement between Kamlet–Jacobs and full Chapman–Jouguet thermochemical-equilibrium codes (EXPLO5, Sucesk\á et al.; Cheetah-2, Fried–Howard) on high-N CHNO compounds: published benchmarks place this disagreement in the 0.3–1.5 km/s band for typical organic explosives and at the higher end (1–2 km/s) for compounds with N-fraction \(\gtrsim 0.4\), where the K-J fixed-product-distribution assumption breaks down. Our population-level §5.13 D9 evidence (Pearson \(r(f_N, \text{residual})=+0.43\), \(p<10^{-26}\)) is itself a regime-aware reproduction of the same effect on 575 Tier-A experimental rows. Definitive absolute-\(D\) numbers for the L1 lead therefore require either an EXPLO5 or Cheetah recompute on the calibrated DFT inputs or experimental synthesis; the 3D-CNN \(D\) should be read as relative-ranking-grade, not absolute-value-grade.

7. Conclusion

A frozen LIMO encoder and a small conditional-DDPM in latent space, conditioned through a four-tier label hierarchy and re-ranked by a 3D-CNN smoke-model + SA/SCScore + Tanimoto-window pipeline, produces hundreds of novel CHNO candidates with predicted detonation properties at or above HMX. The dominant remaining issue is a training–sampling distribution mismatch in the LIMO decoder which a noise-augmentation curriculum is expected to close; with that, the pipeline becomes end-to-end self-consistent at the level of individual seed molecules. The full registry of attempts, including the failure modes, is provided as Appendix C and as a runnable artefact in the source repository.

A. Data preprocessing details

The labelled master is built by canonicalising every input SMILES with RDKit, dropping rows where canonicalisation fails or the molecule is charged after canonicalisation (we ban net charges), and aggregating per-molecule label rows so that each molecule carries the highest-tier value available for each of (density, HOF, det-velocity, det-pressure). Tier ordering is A > B > C > D. Heat-of-formation values are reported in kJ/mol; densities in g/cm3; velocities in km/s; pressures in GPa. We drop molecules with more than 72 SELFIES tokens (the LIMO max) and molecules whose token vocabulary falls outside the LIMO 108-token alphabet. The motif-augmented expansion exhaustively substitutes nitro, nitramine, azide, cyano, tetrazole, and oxadiazole groups onto the labelled scaffolds, then re-canonicalises and re-deduplicates.
