## Methodological concerns

- L309 (§4.9): The anneal formula is defined as $\alpha(\sigma_t) = \max(0, 1 - \sigma_t/\sigma_{\max})$ and the same paragraph then states "production therefore disables the anneal ($\sigma_{\max}=0$, so $\alpha(\sigma_t) \equiv 1$)". With $\sigma_{\max}=0$ the ratio is undefined (or $+\infty$), so $\max(0, 1 - \infty) = 0$, not 1. The intent is clearly "anneal off, $\alpha \equiv 1$" but the literal substitution gives the opposite. Either the disable convention is $\sigma_{\max}=\infty$ (so $\sigma_t/\sigma_{\max} \to 0$), or the formula needs a guard ("if $\sigma_{\max}=0$ then $\alpha\equiv 1$"). The description as written is internally inconsistent and a careful reader will be confused; this affects reproducibility of the production sampler.

- L309 (§4.9): "natural gradient magnitudes at low $\sigma$ are 8–30, well above 5, so the per-head scales $s_h$ have negligible effect." The conclusion that loosening the clamp to $C_g=50$ then makes $s_h$ effective is asserted but not shown to follow numerically; if natural magnitudes peak at ~30, $C_g=50$ is no longer binding and per-head scales become linear, fine, but if magnitudes ever exceed 50 the same saturation argument applies. No calibration of $C_g=50$ vs the empirical max-norm distribution is offered. Appendix D.5 is referenced but only said to log per-step gradient norms; no histogram or quantile cut is reported in the body or appendix prose I read.

- L264 / L758 (§4.6, B.1): The Random Forest viability classifier is trained on (energetic = positive, ZINC drug-like = negative) and Validation AUC = 0.9986 (Table B.1c). An AUC that high on a "drug-like vs energetic" two-class problem is essentially a chemotype-membership classifier, not a viability discriminator — it generalises poorly to the latent gap regions, which is exactly the failure mode self-distillation is meant to patch. This is acknowledged at L758 ("RF generalises poorly"), but the 0.9986 number is reported in B.1c without that caveat and reads as evidence of head quality.

- L274–L276 (§4.7): The score model trains six heads jointly with multi-task loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{score}} = \sum_k a_k \cdot w_k \cdot \mathcal{L}_k$, and the §4.12 paragraph (L343) says "$w_k$ are static head weights chosen so each $\mathcal{L}_k$ sits at $\mathcal{O}(1)$ at convergence". No table reports the actual numerical $w_k$ values; B.1b only says "(values in Table B.1b)" but the table I read does not include a $w_k$ column. This is a methodological-soundness gap: the multi-task balance is unverifiable without the values, and the SA-axis "13% worsening" claim depends on which head dominates the trunk.

- L289 vs L744 (§4.8 vs B.1): Round-0 mining produces 137 hard negatives but L289 says "round-0 training uses 0 hard negatives". The mining step is then arguably part of round 0's *output*, not round 1's *input*; L744 ("round 1 trains on corpus + 137 cheats and mines up to 918 cumulative") is consistent, but the prose at L289 mixes "training round" and "mining round" indices — the 137 number is a round-0 yield used as round-1 training input. ## Suspicious factual claims

- L215 (§4.2): "Reconstruction accuracy (fraction of latent round-trips that reproduce the input SMILES exactly) is 31.4% on the energetic validation set, consistent with the expected LIMO performance in this domain." 31.4% exact-match round-trip is *low* for a frozen-VAE-as-diffusion-prior pipeline. If 68.6% of decoded SMILES differ from input, the diffusion model is being trained against a $\mu$ that does not faithfully encode the molecule. This does not invalidate the recipe (LIMO trained on diverse SMILES is known to have low exact-round-trip), but the "consistent with expected" claim has no citation backing the "expected" baseline. What is the LIMO 2022 reported number on a comparable corpus?

- L215: "$\|\mu\|\approx 8$ on average, well below the $\sqrt{1024}\approx 32$ expected of $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$". $\mathbb{E}\|z\|$ for $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_{1024})$ is approximately $\sqrt{2}\cdot\Gamma(1024/2 + 1/2)/\Gamma(1024/2) \approx \sqrt{1024 - 0.5} \approx 31.99$ — fine. The factor-4 contraction is real. The claim is correct.

- L309: "(natural gradient magnitudes at low $\sigma$ are 8–30, well above 5, so the per-head scales $s_h$ have negligible effect)". This argument is given as the *motivation* for $C_g=50$, but if the natural max is 30, then $C_g=50$ never bites and per-head scales are linear in $s_h$. Why not $C_g = 40$? The factor of 5/3 over the empirical max suggests the clamp is set as a safety buffer, not from the data, but the prose presents it as a derived value.

- L807 (§C.3): "the GMTKN55 thermochemistry benchmarks of Goerigk et al. place the systematic atomization-energy bias of B3LYP with a double-zeta polarised basis at approximately 5–15 kJ/mol per atom". This is high — Goerigk 2017 reports B3LYP/def2-TZVP atomization-energy MAEs in the 5–10 kJ/mol *per molecule* range on GMTKN55 main-group sets, not 5–15 kJ/mol *per atom*. Per-atom error of 5–15 kJ/mol on a 20-atom molecule = 100–300 kJ/mol, which is roughly the +207 kJ/mol calibration offset, so the *result* is consistent, but the *attribution* to per-atom error of that magnitude on a benchmark known for 5–10 kJ/mol per-molecule MAEs is internally inconsistent with how Goerigk is normally cited. Re-check the per-atom vs per-molecule unit.

- L803 (§C.1): "B3LYP/6-31G(d) overestimates N-heteroatom bond lengths by ~0.01–0.02 Å relative to experimental crystal geometries, contributing to the elevated calibration slope (1.392)". A 1–2% bond-length over-estimate gives roughly a 3–6% volume over-estimate at most, hence a 3–6% density *under-estimate* — which would push the calibration slope above 1, consistent with the observed 1.392. Direction is right; magnitude (1.392 vs ideal 1.0 = 39% slope, far above 3–6%) is not explained by this mechanism alone. The dominant source is more plausibly the fixed pk = 0.69 vs the actual crystal-specific packing, which the C.3 discussion already covers; attributing it to bond-length error in C.1 weakens the C.3 narrative.

- L944 (§C.10): "the unrelaxed-fragment xTB scan over-binds slightly relative to a fully relaxed $\omega$B97X-D3BJ recompute, but the chemistry assignment and the weakest-bond ranking are correct". An *unrelaxed* fragment BDE always *under-binds* the bond (i.e., gives a BDE that is too high, because the fragments cannot relax to lower-energy geometries), not *over-binds*. The polarity of the systematic error is reversed. If the protocol gives BDEs above the relaxed value, the appropriate language is "over-estimates BDE / under-binds the cleavage" — pick one and be consistent. L599 also says BDE values are "mildly over-estimated" which is correct given unrelaxed fragments.

- L928 (§C.7): The +0.54 km/s residual in the [0.55–1.00] N-fraction bin is described as a "sign flip" and an "artifact of the open-form Q approximation". Flagged correctly but if the sign flip is artefactual then the Pearson r = +0.43 across the full range is contaminated by it; recomputing r on the 0–0.55 range only would give a more honest single-sign correlation. The body claim that "the residual flips sign" is then somewhat mis-leading because the closed-form K-J in Table C.2 does not show that flip on the leads (all 12 leads have K-J under-prediction).
